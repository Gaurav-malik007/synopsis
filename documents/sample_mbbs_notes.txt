ANATOMY - THE HEART
====================

Introduction
The heart is a muscular organ located in the mediastinum of the thoracic cavity, slightly to the left of the midline. It is roughly the size of a closed fist and weighs approximately 250-350 grams in adults. The heart is the central organ of the cardiovascular system, responsible for pumping blood throughout the body.

Pericardium
The heart is enclosed within a double-walled sac called the pericardium. The pericardium has two layers:
1. Fibrous pericardium - the tough outer layer that anchors the heart to surrounding structures
2. Serous pericardium - has two sublayers:
   a. Parietal layer - lines the fibrous pericardium
   b. Visceral layer (epicardium) - adheres to the heart surface
The pericardial cavity between these layers contains a small amount of pericardial fluid (15-50 mL) which reduces friction during heartbeats.

Chambers of the Heart
The heart has four chambers:
- Right Atrium (RA): Receives deoxygenated blood from the body via the superior vena cava (SVC) and inferior vena cava (IVC). The coronary sinus also drains into the RA.
- Right Ventricle (RV): Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary trunk. Has thinner walls compared to the left ventricle.
- Left Atrium (LA): Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via four pulmonary veins (two from each lung).
- Left Ventricle (LV): Pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body via the aorta. Has the thickest walls (approximately 3 times thicker than RV) due to the high pressure needed for systemic circulation.

Valves of the Heart
The heart has four valves that ensure unidirectional blood flow:

Atrioventricular (AV) Valves:
- Tricuspid valve: Between the right atrium and right ventricle (has 3 cusps)
- Mitral valve (Bicuspid valve): Between the left atrium and left ventricle (has 2 cusps)
Both AV valves are attached to papillary muscles via chordae tendineae, which prevent valve prolapse during ventricular contraction.

Semilunar Valves:
- Pulmonary valve: Between the right ventricle and pulmonary trunk (has 3 semilunar cusps)
- Aortic valve: Between the left ventricle and aorta (has 3 semilunar cusps)

Clinical Correlation: Mitral valve prolapse (MVP) is one of the most common valvular disorders, affecting 2-3% of the population. It occurs when the mitral valve leaflets bulge into the left atrium during systole.

Blood Supply to the Heart
The heart receives its own blood supply through the coronary arteries:

Right Coronary Artery (RCA):
- Arises from the right aortic sinus
- Supplies the right atrium, right ventricle, SA node (in 60% of people), AV node (in 80% of people)
- Gives rise to the posterior descending artery (PDA) in right-dominant circulation (85% of people)

Left Coronary Artery (LCA):
- Arises from the left aortic sinus
- Divides into two main branches:
  1. Left Anterior Descending (LAD) artery - supplies the anterior wall of both ventricles and the anterior 2/3 of the interventricular septum
  2. Left Circumflex (LCx) artery - supplies the left atrium and lateral wall of the left ventricle

Clinical Correlation: The LAD is sometimes called the "widow maker" because its occlusion leads to a large area of myocardial infarction affecting the anterior wall and septum, which can be fatal.

Venous Drainage:
- Most venous blood drains into the coronary sinus, which opens into the right atrium
- Small cardiac veins (venae cordis minimae / Thebesian veins) drain directly into the chambers

Conducting System of the Heart
The heart has its own intrinsic conducting system that generates and propagates electrical impulses:

1. SA Node (Sinoatrial Node): Located in the wall of the right atrium near the SVC opening. Acts as the natural pacemaker, generating impulses at 60-100 beats per minute.

2. AV Node (Atrioventricular Node): Located in the interatrial septum near the coronary sinus opening. Provides a brief delay (0.1 second) allowing atrial contraction to complete before ventricular contraction begins.

3. Bundle of His: Passes through the fibrous skeleton of the heart from the AV node to the interventricular septum.

4. Bundle Branches: The Bundle of His divides into:
   - Right bundle branch - travels down the right side of the interventricular septum
   - Left bundle branch - divides into anterior and posterior fascicles

5. Purkinje Fibers: Terminal branches that spread throughout the ventricular myocardium, enabling rapid and coordinated ventricular contraction.

Clinical Correlation: Heart blocks occur when conduction is impaired at various levels. A complete (third-degree) heart block at the AV node results in complete dissociation between atrial and ventricular rhythms and may require a pacemaker.

Nerve Supply
The heart receives both sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation:

Sympathetic: From T1-T4 spinal segments via cardiac accelerator nerves
- Increases heart rate (positive chronotropy)
- Increases force of contraction (positive inotropy)
- Increases conduction velocity (positive dromotropy)

Parasympathetic: Via the vagus nerve (CN X)
- Decreases heart rate (negative chronotropy)
- Decreases conduction velocity through AV node
- Minimal effect on ventricular contractility

Clinical Correlation: Referred pain from the heart (as in myocardial infarction) is felt in the T1-T4 dermatomes - this explains why cardiac pain can radiate to the left arm, jaw, and epigastrium.

Surface Anatomy
The borders of the heart can be mapped on the chest wall:
- Right border: Right 3rd costal cartilage to right 6th costal cartilage (formed by RA)
- Inferior border: Right 6th costal cartilage to the apex (formed mainly by RV)
- Left border: Left 2nd costal cartilage to the apex (formed mainly by LV)
- Superior border: Lower borders of the 2nd costal cartilages (formed by both atria and great vessels)
- Apex: Located in the left 5th intercostal space, in the midclavicular line (formed by LV)

The apex beat is normally palpable in the left 5th intercostal space at the midclavicular line.
